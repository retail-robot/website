Thoughts on software development

A question I'm asked a lot by parents is "how do I raise my kid to become a developer?"

(Quickly this is followed up by an "I bet you were really good at Math". An underlying assumption being that math is really important. It has been to some extent, but I was middling at Math growing up (and still am). I could follow the lessons, and pass the classes well enough but I don't think they really stuck all too well with me. I'm no mathematician.

I think most of my success as a developer came from the way I was taught things - be it math, or science, or writing, or music. It was not the subjects in and of themselves, but the way I was taught to engage with the world around me, to seek out knowledge, and how to evaluate my understanding of the world. Systems thinking and critical thinking are the skills I value most about myself. The ability not just to craft a hypothesis, but to test it requires you to think deeply about the truth of things. How do you prove something? (You need to be able to repeat it). You need to be able to think critically to write and test a hypothesis.

My literature classes taught me to think in lenses - systems of meaning. At the time it seemed arbitrary, but looking back it taught me a blunt framework to look at things with more than one point of view. I remember disliking it because it seemed silly to only apply analysis from one lens, but looking back, even that was teaching me the value of systems thinking - divide and conquer. Analyze things in their simplest state, and combine the analysis later.

Most good software requires the writer to model an entire domain of information. To some extent, you are discerning what is important to model, what is practical to model, and where performance is a limiting factor, what you can cut or elide about that model without undercutting the usefulness of a program's output.
